Iraq's trajectory since 2003 has been one of the most turbulent nation-building experiments in modern history—a story of destruction, partial recovery, and persistent fragility.
The Arc of Violence and Stability
The immediate post-invasion years (2003-2007) were catastrophic. The disbanding of the Iraqi army, de-Baathification, and the power vacuum unleashed a multi-sided civil war—Sunni insurgency, Shia militias, al-Qaeda in Iraq, and sectarian cleansing that killed hundreds of thousands and displaced millions. Baghdad neighborhoods that had been mixed for generations became mono-sectarian.
The US surge and the Sunni Awakening (2007-2008) brought temporary stability, but the underlying political settlement remained broken. Nouri al-Maliki's increasingly sectarian governance alienated Sunnis, creating the conditions for ISIS to sweep through a third of the country in 2014. The recapture of Mosul in 2017 came at enormous cost—entire cities reduced to rubble.
Since 2017, violence has dropped dramatically by Iraqi standards. There's no active insurgency controlling territory. But the state never fully reasserted its monopoly on violence. Iran-backed militias (the Popular Mobilization Forces) became parallel power structures, sometimes stronger than the formal military.
The Political Settlement That Isn't
Iraq operates under a consociational system—the presidency goes to Kurds, the prime ministership to Shia, the speakership to Sunnis. In theory this ensures representation; in practice it's calcified into elite cartel arrangements where parties divide ministries as patronage fiefdoms rather than governing institutions.
The 2019 Tishreen protests were genuinely remarkable—young Iraqis, primarily Shia, rejecting the entire sectarian framework and Iranian influence. Hundreds were killed by security forces and militias. The protests extracted some concessions (a new electoral law, early elections) but didn't fundamentally alter the system. The political class waited them out.
Muqtada al-Sadr's dramatic withdrawal from politics in 2022 after winning the most seats but failing to form a government illustrated how dysfunctional the system has become—even the largest bloc couldn't break the gridlock imposed by Iran-aligned factions demanding their share.
The Economic Picture
Iraq remains a textbook case of the resource curse. Oil accounts for roughly 95% of government revenue and 99% of exports. When oil prices are high, the government can buy social peace through public sector employment (the state employs perhaps a third of the workforce) and subsidies. When prices crash, as in 2014-2016 and 2020, the fiscal situation becomes desperate.
GDP per capita has recovered from the ISIS-era collapse and sits around $5,000-6,000—middle-income by global standards but far below Iraq's potential given its oil wealth. The private sector outside oil is anemic. Electricity remains unreliable despite billions spent on generation capacity (much of it lost to corruption and militia-controlled smuggling networks). Youth unemployment hovers around 25-30%.
The currency devaluation in late 2020 and the subsequent US pressure on dollar flows through Iraqi banks (to curb Iranian sanctions evasion) created significant economic turbulence. The dinar's black market rate diverged sharply from the official rate, squeezing importers and ordinary Iraqis.
International Position
Iraq has settled into an awkward but somewhat functional balancing act. It maintains relations with the US (which still has troops there in an advisory role) while being deeply penetrated by Iranian influence through militias, political parties, and economic ties. It has decent relations with Gulf states, particularly Saudi Arabia, which has been courting Baghdad as a potential counterweight to Tehran.
The Abraham Accords and regional de-escalation (the Saudi-Iran rapprochement) have actually helped Iraq by reducing the intensity of the proxy competition being fought on its soil. Iraq has positioned itself as a potential mediator and meeting ground—Baghdad hosted Saudi-Iranian talks.
Kurdistan remains semi-autonomous with its own oil exports, military, and foreign relations, though its leverage has diminished since the failed 2017 independence referendum led to loss of Kirkuk and a financial crackdown from Baghdad.
The Direction
The honest assessment is: muddle through. Iraq is no longer in freefall, but it's not building toward anything coherent either. The political class has no incentive to reform a system that enriches them. The population is exhausted and cynical after two decades of violence and disappointment. Young people who can leave, do.
Climate change poses an existential threat—Iraq is one of the most water-stressed countries on earth, dependent on the Tigris and Euphrates whose flows are increasingly controlled by Turkish and Iranian dams. Summer temperatures are becoming literally unsurvivable in parts of the south.
The optimistic case: oil revenues remain high enough to maintain the patronage system, violence stays low, and gradual institution-building occurs over decades. The pessimistic case: another oil crash, another militia-sparked conflict with the US or regional actors, or climate-driven agricultural collapse triggers renewed instability.
What Iraq hasn't achieved, and shows little sign of achieving, is the transformation into a functional state with a diversified economy, accountable governance, and a shared national identity that transcends sect and ethnicity. It exists, it functions at a basic level, but it doesn't thrive.
