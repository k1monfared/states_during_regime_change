Syria's trajectory since Assad's fall in December 2024 has been surprisingly—perhaps precariously—more hopeful than most analysts expected, though deeply fragile and marked by serious violence.
The Collapse Itself
The speed was stunning. A lightning 11-day offensive in late November and early December 2024, led by Hay'at Tahrir al-Sham (HTS) and supported by the Turkish-backed Syrian National Army, swept through the country. Al Jazeera On December 8, 2024, Assad fled to Russia, where he was granted asylum, ending 53 years of Assad family rule. Wikipedia The regime simply evaporated—there was no Alawite redoubt, no prolonged last stand in the coastal heartland. Assad had reportedly planned to establish an Alawite state on the Syrian coast as a fallback, but it never materialized partly because of mixed attitudes among Alawites themselves toward HTS. Wikipedia
Political Consolidation Under HTS
Ahmed al-Sharaa (formerly known as Abu Mohammed al-Julani) moved quickly to consolidate power. By January 29, 2025, he was appointed president for the transitional period, and on March 13, a Constitutional Declaration granted him sweeping executive authority for a five-year transition. American University The Ba'ath Party was dissolved on January 30—a highly symbolic break with the past. Al Jazeera
The transition has been more orderly than expected from a former al-Qaeda affiliate. HTS has assured protection for Christians and other minorities, and al-Sharaa met with senior Syrian Christian leaders at the People's Palace in late December. Wikipedia There have been positive steps on justice and transparency, including the creation of a National Commission for Transitional Justice Human Rights Watch—though critics note its mandate is limited to crimes by the Assad government, making transitional justice one-sided. Human Rights Watch
The Violence That Hasn't Stopped
The most serious shadow over the transition has been sectarian violence. In March 2025, Syrian security forces and pro-government fighters committed a massacre of more than 740 Alawite civilians during clashes in western Syria. Wikipedia France condemned atrocities committed against civilians based on their religious faith or community affiliation on the Syrian coast in March 2025, and in the Sweida region (Druze) in August 2025. Ministère de l'Europe et des Affaires étrangères
Christian communities have also faced renewed threats, including the burning of a Christmas tree in Suqaylabiyah by Islamist fighters and an attack on a church in Hama. Georgetown Journal of International Affairs
These episodes represent exactly the kind of post-regime sectarian score-settling that characterized Iraq after 2003—though so far on a smaller scale.
The Kurdish Question
Turkey has used the transition to press its longstanding campaign against Kurdish forces. The Turkish-backed SNA launched Operation Dawn of Freedom, capturing Tel Rifaat and pushing toward Kobani. Turkish and SNA bombardment of SDF-controlled territories has continued into 2025. Wikipedia
However, in a significant development, the SDF agreed to integrate into state institutions on March 10, 2025 Wikipedia—a potential resolution to what seemed like an intractable problem, though implementation remains uncertain.
Israel's Expanded Footprint
Israel has occupied parts of southern Syria around the Golan Heights for an "unlimited amount of time" and has conducted air strikes targeting Assad-era military facilities. House of Commons Library Analysts note Israel may be aiming to maintain a divided Syria through supporting minority communities to capitalize on Iran's loss of its ally. House of Commons Library
Economic Trajectory
The economy inherited by the new government was catastrophic. Over 13 years of civil war, the Syrian economy declined by over 50%, and the currency lost 99% of its value. House of Commons Library Following a contraction of 1.5% in 2024, Syria's GDP is expected to grow modestly by 1% in 2025. World Bank
The key development has been the lifting of international sanctions. On May 13, 2025, President Trump announced lifting U.S. sanctions on Syria. In June, Executive Order 14312 formally removed U.S. sanctions. U.S. Department of State The EU adopted legal acts lifting all economic restrictive measures on May 28, 2025. Consilium Syria has been able to export oil for the first time in 14 years. House of Commons Library
Reconstruction costs are staggering. The World Bank estimated reconstruction costs at over $216 billion, and notes this is likely an underestimate. House of Commons Library Syrian Minister of Economy Mohammed al-Shaar claimed "at least $1 trillion" was needed. Carnegie Endowment for International Peace
There's concern that without clarity on the economic framework, Assad-era cronyism may reappear—some state assets are being slated for privatization with little transparency regarding beneficiaries. Carnegie Endowment for International Peace
Refugee Returns
More than one million refugees have returned since December 2024, alongside some two million internally displaced who have gone back to their communities. UN News This is remarkable speed, driven by a combination of hope and pressure from host countries (particularly Turkey, which hosted 3.7 million at peak) that want to send refugees back.
International Reintegration
France and Germany sent their foreign ministers to Damascus in January 2025—the first visit by top European officials since Assad's fall. A Paris Conference on Syria in February brought together 19 countries, the EU, the UN, the Arab League, and the Gulf Cooperation Council. Ministère de l'Europe et des Affaires étrangères The UK formally established diplomatic ties in July 2025. House of Commons Library
Al-Sharaa made his first foreign trip to Saudi Arabia in February 2025 to meet Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman Al Jazeera, signaling the Gulf's interest in the new Syria.
The Direction
The honest assessment a year on is: better than expected, but still precarious. HTS has governed more pragmatically than its jihadi origins would suggest. Sanctions relief and diplomatic normalization have proceeded faster than anyone anticipated. Refugee returns are happening.
But the warning signs are serious: sectarian massacres, one-sided transitional justice, concentration of power in one leader with a five-year mandate, no clear constitutional process, ongoing Turkish military operations against Kurds, Israeli occupation of Syrian territory, ISIS still present and capable, and an economy that will take decades to rebuild even with everything going right.
As Human Rights Watch put it on the one-year anniversary: "growing signs that the window of opportunity may be closing." Human Rights Watch
The comparison to Iraq is instructive. Syria has avoided the worst of Iraq's post-2003 trajectory—no complete state collapse, no full-scale civil war, no mass sectarian cleansing at Iraqi scales. But it also hasn't established the foundations for genuine pluralism or accountability. The question is whether the relative stability holds long enough for reconstruction to create stakeholders in peace, or whether the unresolved tensions explode into another round of conflict.
